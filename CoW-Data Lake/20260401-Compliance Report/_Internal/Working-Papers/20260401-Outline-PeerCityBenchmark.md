## I. Why CoW Needs Peer Benchmarking
**Input source:** Known from kickoff (Oct 2025) and proposal context — draftable now
**Drafting prerequisite:** None

Three reasons CoW asked for this research, stated plainly:

1. Standards cannot be selected in a vacuum. CoW needs to know whether the framework we recommend is one that comparable cities have actually implemented — not just one that looks good on paper.
2. CoW is not the first city to face these problems. Ungoverned storage on USB drives and retention schedules that exist on paper but are not enforced are documented problems in municipal data governance. Other cities have solved them. The question is how, how long it took, and what it cost.
3. Leadership buy-in requires precedent. A recommendation grounded in "here is what Austin did and what changed" is more actionable than a framework specification alone.

**Scope of this section in the final deliverable:** 1 short paragraph. This is not a section heading in the final brief — it is context for the reader before the findings begin.

## V. How Peer Cities Solved CoW's Two Core Problems
**Input source:** Peer city research (Weeks 2–3) + challenge-specific secondary research
**Drafting prerequisite:** Challenge research complete; at least one peer city example per challenge

### 5.1 Shadow Data — Ungoverned Storage on USB Drives and Local Hard Drives

CoW's specific situation: Records exist in Laserfiche Cloud and in 45 Excel data management lists. An undocumented volume also exists on USB drives, personal hard drives, and shared network drives outside any governed system. This is a records compliance risk that cannot be closed through policy alone.

Structure for this section in the final deliverable:
- What at least one peer city did to detect the scope of the problem (inventory method)
- What policy instrument they used to prohibit ungoverned storage
- Whether they used an amnesty window or immediate enforcement for migration
- What technology controls (if any) they deployed
- Realistic timeline from policy adoption to measurable compliance

[RESEARCH REQUIRED — draft from peer findings]

### 5.2 Unenforced Retention Schedules

CoW's specific situation: 33 retention periods and 8 cutoff triggers are defined in the data management lists. The legal citation source has not been confirmed. Retention enforcement automation in Laserfiche is unverified. No disposition approval workflow is documented.

Structure for this section in the final deliverable:
- How at least one peer city established or rebuilt its retention schedule from an inconsistent baseline
- What serves as the legal anchor (state law, city charter, City Clerk policy, or combination)
- How they automated enforcement in their DMS — triggers, workflows, approval steps
- How they handled legacy records with no retention metadata
- What roles City Clerk, Legal, and IT each played

## VII. Limitations
**Input source:** Known constraints — draftable now
**Drafting prerequisite:** None; update after research if new constraints surface

This section appears in the final client deliverable.

- Peer city research relies primarily on publicly available documents. Practices not published online may not be captured unless direct outreach to city IT or records staff is successful.
- Research is conducted in Weeks 2–3 concurrently with stakeholder interviews. Depth is limited by the 6-week engagement timeline.
- Findings reflect each city's published governance program as of April 2026. Programs may have evolved since their most recent public documentation.
- Omaha and Tulsa have less publicly documented governance programs than Austin and Kansas City. If primary research yields limited findings for these cities, their entries in the comparative matrix will reflect that constraint rather than speculate.
- Bloomberg WWC certification criteria are published but the internal effort required to achieve certification is not always publicly documented. Feasibility estimates will be based on publicly available city profiles and program timelines where available.
